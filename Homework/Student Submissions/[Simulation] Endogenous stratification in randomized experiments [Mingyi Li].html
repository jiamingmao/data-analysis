HW1¶

  • This homework aims to validate Abadie et al (2018) using simulated dataset

Background¶

  • Abadie et al (2018) argue over-fitting is manifest when researchers divide recipients into different groups to investigate potential heterogeneity in RCT, and propose possible estimators to alleviate such bias. Specifically,
  • Findings
    • Over-fitting appears in ATE when stratification is based on recipients own characteristics.
    • In high score group, ATE is underestimated, whereas ATE is overestimated in low score group.
    • Famous JTPA and STAR experiments both incur ATE bias described above.
    • Using leave-one-out or repeated split sample estimators can address over-fitting and obtain consistent coefficients.

Methodology¶

  • We generate random sample following Normal distribution with 10,000 simulations and randomly group them into control and treatment groups
  • In our DGP, samples are randomly selected into treatment group and control group, hence, ATE estimator is supposed to be indistinguishable from 0
  • Specifically, we consider small sample properties and large sample properties in our simulations

Results¶

  • Figure 1 presents small sample ATE estimates stratified by score of covariates, it manifests that
    • Low score group has an overestimated tendency (+7.8)
    • High score group has an underestimated tendency (-7.2)
    • These deviations are about 700% of mean of y
  • Figure 2 presents small sample repeated split sample ATE estimates stratified by score of covariates, the pattern of underestimation and overestimation is almost indistinguishable.
  • Figure 3 and 4 present large sample ATE estimates based on overall sample and repeated split sample estimators, respectively. In large sample, the bias we find in small sample case is greatly alleviated.
    • In Figure 3. Low score group tends to overestimate ATE, and high score group tends to underestimate ATE, whereas the magnitude is almost indistinguishable.
    • In Figure 4. None of groups tends to biase, similar with Figure 2.
  • Overall. repeated split sample estimator can greatly alleviate the ATE bias induced by endogenous stratification regardless of small of large samples.

Reference¶

Abadie, A., Chingos, M. M., and West, M. R. (2018). Endogenous stratification in randomized experiments. Review of Economics and Statistics, 100(4), 567-580.

# We run 10,000 simulations with 100 samples to show small sample properties of baseline estimate
for epoch in range(10000):
    # Features:(100,40). i.i.d. Standard Normal
    X = np.random.normal(loc=0, scale= 1,size=(100,40))
    # True coefficients, all betas = 1
    b = np.ones(40)
    # Error, Normal(0,60)
    e = np.random.normal(loc=0, scale= np.sqrt(60), size = (100,1))
    # By Normal additivity, y follows Normal(1, 100)
    y = 1 + np.expand_dims(X.dot(b),axis=1) + e
    X = pd.DataFrame(X, columns=columns)
    y = pd.DataFrame(y, columns=['y'])
    # Randomly divide total sample into Control group (training group) and Treat Group (Test group)
    X_train,X_test = X.iloc[:50,:],X.iloc[50:,:]
    y_train,y_test = y.iloc[:50,:],y.iloc[50:,:]
    
    # We use all relevant Xs to estimate and it is readily to use other numbers of features here.
    # Use all training sample to estimate our linear model
    model40 = LinearRegression()
    model40.fit(X_train, y_train)
    
    y_train_pred = model40.predict(X_train)
    # X_res stores all features and estimated y
    # y_res stores real y and estimated y
    # Estimated y is employed as a sorting variable
    X_res = pd.concat([X_train,pd.DataFrame(y_train_pred,columns=['y_pred'])],axis=1)
    y_res = pd.concat([y_train,pd.DataFrame(y_train_pred,columns=['y_pred'])],axis=1)
    y_res = y_res.sort_values(by='y_pred')

    
    # Predict y in test sample
    y_test_pred = pd.DataFrame(model40.predict(X_test),columns=['y_pred'])
    # Similarly, concatenate estimated y in test group
    X_test_res = pd.DataFrame(np.concatenate((X_test,y_test_pred),axis=1),columns=X_res.columns)
    y_test_res = pd.DataFrame(np.concatenate((y_test,y_test_pred),axis=1),columns=y_res.columns)
    y_test_res = y_test_res.sort_values(by='y_pred')
    
    # We use predicted value using all available features to sort observations into three equally spiltted groups
    # based on their estimated y, and appending estimated ATE in corresponding lists
    low_bs.append(y_test_res.iloc[:16,:].mean()[0] - y_res.iloc[:16,:].mean()[0])
    mid_bs.append(y_test_res.iloc[16: 16 * 2,:].mean()[0] - y_res.iloc[16: 16 * 2,:].mean()[0])
    high_bs.append(y_test_res.iloc[16*2:,:].mean()[0] - y_res.iloc[16*2:,:].mean()[0])
